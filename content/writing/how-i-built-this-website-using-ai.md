## A Journey into Modern Web Development

I take pride in my work. As a web developer, the portfolio website is a portfolio piece within itself. It's a reflection of your skills, design sensibilities, and attention to detail. For importantly, it's a way to showcase your perfect project based not on client requests or business requirements but on your own vision and creativity. For the past iterations of my website, I have always designed and built from scratch, which has been a rewarding experience but also time-consuming. This time around, I wanted to try something different and use this latest version to showcase my AI skills.

Since most of the work I do these days are for large companies, I unfortunately don't have an opportunity to share the projects I work on. With this website however, I can showcase my AI skills and share the process of how I built it. You can be the judge of if I am levering these modern tools effectively.

## Expanding on My Original Experience
In 2019, I built my second version of my portfolio website using Nuxt.js. All of these components were hand crafted by me including the design and page animations. I wanted to create a unique experience for visitors and provide a fun interactive experience. The whole idea of letting the user select their color theme started with me adjusting the color of my plaid shirt in Photoshop to red.

As I was sliding the hue adjustment slider back and forth, I saw a few plaid shirt colors that I liked. I saved each of these in the `public` directory and tested each one out in-browser. Then it came it me, why not let th user select their color theme? It's such a simple action but it (to me) is incredibly satisfying. It was around this time that I started teaching at the University of Cincinnati. I often would show my students my portfolio website as an example of how these skills can be used to build something unique. I got a lot of "wow!" and "Ohh cool!" reactions. So I figured I had something at least.

### Keeping Up with the Times
For this new verison, I wanted to keep that "theme selector" feature but wanted to make it more robust and more of a traditional website. In addition, the software engineering/web development __field has changed tremendously__ since 2019.
