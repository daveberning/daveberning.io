Few questions in web development generate as much unnecessary debate as this one. Should your site live at `www.example.com` or `example.com`? Does it affect your search rankings? Will Google penalize you for choosing the wrong one? This article on Medium cuts through the noise with a clear answer.

**Google does not care which version you choose.** It treats `www` and non-`www` URLs as separate websites, indexes each independently, and ranks them based on the same signals it uses for everything else — traffic, engagement, backlinks, content quality. Neither format has a built-in advantage over the other in Google's algorithm.

The real-world evidence backs this up. Facebook, Apple, and YouTube all use the `www` prefix. GitHub, Twitter, and CodePen do not. All of them rank just fine. The prefix is a technical convention, not an SEO lever.

**Consistency is the only rule that matters.** Google's concern isn't which version you pick — it's whether you pick one and stick with it. If some of your pages are served at `www` and others aren't, Google sees them as two separate sites competing for the same rankings. Backlinks split between both versions dilute your authority rather than consolidating it. Redirecting one version permanently to the other, and making sure every internal link points to the canonical version, is all it takes to stay clean.

Pick whichever format fits your preference or your team's convention, set up a 301 redirect from the other, and move on. There are more consequential SEO decisions to spend time on.
